What these numbers mean: A 27% conviction rate does not indicate false cases — it indicates systemic failure to prosecute. Police non-registration, witness intimidation, delayed investigation, and judicial pendency all contribute. The "misuse" narrative is contradicted by these numbers: if the Act were being weaponised, conviction rates would be higher, not lower.

Sexual violence against Dalit women

Caste-based sexual violence operates differently from general sexual violence. It is often used as an instrument of caste assertion — to punish Dalit communities that challenge dominant-caste authority, to humiliate Dalit men through their women, and to reinforce hierarchies of purity and pollution.

Dalit women are underrepresented in police forces, lack access to legal aid, face hostile local administration, and are frequently pressured to reach out-of-court "settlements" with perpetrators. The caste identity of the perpetrator determines the response of the state.

The Hathras case (2020): A 19-year-old Dalit woman was gang-raped and murdered in Hathras, UP. Police cremated her body at night without family consent — destroying forensic evidence. The UP government deployed 4,000 personnel to prevent protests. The accused were acquitted in 2021. The Special Investigation Team's report was sealed by the Allahabad High Court.

The Indian police force is structurally upper-caste in its officer cadre. Studies by sociologist Vivek Kumar and others document how caste identity shapes police response — Dalit FIRs are routinely not registered, Dalit complainants are coerced to withdraw, and Dalit accused receive harsher treatment. The near-zero conviction rate for police officers in custodial death cases reflects judicial deference to state authority.

Documented cases

These are cases that reached national attention. They represent a fraction of what happens daily. Each is followed by its judicial status — the most important part.

Hathras Gang Rape & Murder
2020Rape & Murder
A 19-year-old Dalit woman from Hathras, UP, was gang-raped and murdered by four upper-caste men. She died in Delhi's Safdarjung Hospital. Police cremated her body overnight without family consent, destroying forensic evidence. The state government initially denied rape.
Accused acquitted (2021)
Judiciary: Allahabad HC sealed the SIT report. Trial court acquitted all four accused, citing lack of forensic evidence — the same evidence destroyed by police. No officer was held accountable for the illegal cremation.
Rohith Vemula Institutional Killing
2016Institutional Caste
Rohith Vemula, a PhD scholar and Ambedkarite activist at Hyderabad Central University, died by suicide after being suspended along with four other Dalit students following pressure from the BJP student union and the Union Ministry of Education. His letter before death described the dehumanisation of Dalit students.
No conviction · Inquiry ongoing
Judiciary: A magistrate inquiry in 2023 ruled it was not a suicide abetment case. The university administration and political figures who engineered his suspension faced no criminal charges. The SC/ST Act case filed by his mother remains pending.
Unnao Rape Case
2017–2019Rape
A teenage Dalit girl alleged she was raped by BJP MLA Kuldeep Singh Sengar in Unnao, UP. Her father was jailed on false arms charges and died in custody. The victim and her lawyer were later hit by a truck in suspicious circumstances. The CBI took over after Supreme Court intervention.
Convicted — life imprisonment (2019)
Judiciary: The CBI court convicted Sengar. However, it took two years, Supreme Court intervention, and national outcry to reach trial. Multiple police officers who filed false cases against the victim's family were not prosecuted.
Bhima Koregaon Violence
2018Atrocity & State Action
Violence broke out near the Bhima Koregaon war memorial — sacred to Dalits as a site where Mahar soldiers defeated the Peshwa army in 1818. Dalit pilgrims were attacked by Hindutva groups. The Maharashtra government and police, however, arrested 16 academics, lawyers, and activists (the Bhima Koregaon 16) under UAPA, calling them Maoist conspirators.
Accused in jail for 5+ years · Trial ongoing
Judiciary: The accused remain in custody without trial completion. Father Stan Swamy, an 83-year-old Jesuit priest arrested in the case, died in custody in 2021 — denied bail despite severe illness. No one from the groups that attacked Dalit pilgrims has been convicted.
Khairlanji Massacre
2006Murder
Four members of the Bhotmange family — a Dalit family in Khairlanji, Maharashtra — were killed by a mob of upper-caste villagers. The women were stripped and paraded naked before being murdered. The attack followed the family's refusal to leave their land and their support for a Dalit woman in a land dispute.
6 convicted — but SC/ST Act dropped
Judiciary: The sessions court convicted six people but under Indian Penal Code, not SC/ST Act — meaning the caste dimension was legally erased. The Bombay High Court upheld the conviction but reduced sentences. The Supreme Court restored some sentences. No one served the maximum sentence for what was clearly a caste atrocity.
Lakhimpur Kheri — Dalit Girls Found Hanging
2021Suspected Murder
Two Dalit sisters, aged 15 and 17, were found hanging from a tree in Lakhimpur Kheri, UP. Their family alleged murder and sexual assault. Police initially claimed suicide.
Chargesheet filed · Trial ongoing
Judiciary: The case proceeded only after sustained media pressure. Three accused were arrested. Trial is ongoing with significant delays. The pattern — police initially claiming suicide, slow investigation, prolonged trial — is consistent across Dalit cases.

Judiciary delay — the second injustice

In caste atrocity cases, the judicial system functions as a second layer of violence. Even when cases reach trial, delay, acquittal, and procedural obstruction ensure that justice is routinely denied. This is not accidental — it reflects the caste composition of the judiciary.

01
Dedicated courts not functional. The SC/ST Prevention of Atrocities Act mandates dedicated Special Courts for fast-track trials. As of 2022, only 194 of 406 mandated special courts were functional. Cases pile up in regular courts where they wait years for hearing dates.
02
Police non-cooperation sabotages prosecution. The chargesheet quality in SC/ST cases is systematically poor — witnesses not examined, forensic evidence not collected, FIRs delayed. Since police are responsible for investigation and are overwhelmingly dominant-caste, this is structural, not accidental.
03
Witnesses are coerced to turn hostile. In the period between registration and trial (often 5+ years), victims and witnesses face economic pressure, threats, and inducements to retract. In atrocity cases, the accused are often economically and politically dominant — their ability to intimidate witnesses over years is enormous.
04
The 2018 Supreme Court judgment. In Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra, the Supreme Court ruled that a preliminary inquiry was required before FIR registration under the SC/ST Act. This was widely condemned as gutting the law. Though the judgment was partly reversed by a Constitution Bench in 2019, the damage — in terms of police reluctance to register cases — persisted.
05
Caste in the judiciary itself. A 2023 RTI analysis found that over 80% of High Court judges belong to upper castes. There are almost no Dalit judges in the Supreme Court or most High Courts. The judiciary that adjudicates caste atrocity cases is composed overwhelmingly of people from the communities that commit those atrocities.
06
Acquittal used as "proof" of false cases. High acquittal rates in SC/ST Act cases are routinely cited by dominant-caste commentators as evidence of misuse. The causal chain is reversed: acquittals are produced by poor investigation, hostile witnesses, and judicial delay — they are the output of systemic failure, not the input of false complaints.

Manual scavenging was abolished in 1993 and again by the Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers and their Rehabilitation Act, 2013. Despite this, the practice continues — entirely within Dalit, Valmiki, and other lower-caste communities. The 2013 Act has resulted in zero employer convictions in a decade.

Deaths in sewers and septic tanks are classified as "accidents" rather than occupational murders — allowing municipalities and private employers to escape liability. The National Commission for Safai Karmacharis has repeatedly flagged this to government with no systematic response.

Residential segregation by caste is near-total in rural India and significant in urban areas. "Dalit bastis" are physically separated from dominant-caste areas — with separate wells, roads, and often separate temples. This is spatial untouchability: the architecture of caste made concrete.

In cities, Dalit rental discrimination operates through surname identification — the same mechanism as job market discrimination. A 2022 study found landlords 3x more likely to reject enquiries from applicants with Dalit surnames even when income and employment were identical.
