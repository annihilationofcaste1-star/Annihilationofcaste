Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar (1891–1956) — jurist, economist, social reformer, and the principal architect of the Indian Constitution. The most educated leader of his generation and one of the most consequential figures in modern history.

Life & timeline

14 April 1891
Born in Mhow, Central Provinces
Born the 14th child of Ramji Maloji Sakpal and Bhimabai, a Mahar (Dalit) family. His father was an army officer. The family faced severe caste discrimination despite the father's military service — children were made to sit separately in school and were denied water by upper-caste teachers.
1907
Matriculation — a milestone for a Dalit student
Passed matriculation from Bombay University — rare for a Dalit student at the time. His success attracted the attention of Maharaja Sayajirao Gaekwad of Baroda, who would later sponsor his overseas education.
1913
Sails to New York — Columbia University
At age 22, Ambedkar left for Columbia University, New York, on a scholarship from Baroda State. He studied under John Dewey, the philosopher of democracy, who had a profound influence on his thinking about liberty and social equality.
1915–1917
MA and PhD from Columbia University
Completed MA (1915) and PhD (1917) in Economics. His doctoral thesis, "The Evolution of Provincial Finance in British India," was groundbreaking. He then went to London School of Economics and Gray's Inn to study law and economics simultaneously.
1920
Founds Mooknayak newspaper
Launches a fortnightly newspaper Mooknayak (Leader of the Silent) to give voice to Dalit issues. The name referred to the millions of Dalits whose suffering went unspoken in mainstream politics and press.
1927
Mahad Satyagraha — right to drink water
Led the historic Mahad Satyagraha, where thousands of Dalits marched to drink water from the Chavdar Tank — a public water source they were barred from using. He publicly burned the Manusmriti — the Hindu text codifying caste hierarchy — calling it an act of liberation.
1930
Nasik Temple Entry Satyagraha
Led Dalits to assert their right to enter the Kalaram Temple in Nasik. The campaign lasted five years. It exposed the contradiction between Hindu reformers who claimed inclusivity while maintaining caste exclusion in practice.
1935
"I will not die a Hindu"
At the Yeola Conference, Ambedkar publicly declared he would not die a Hindu. He began 21 years of comparative study of world religions — Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, and Buddhism — before choosing Buddhism as the vehicle for Dalit liberation.
1936
Writes Annihilation of Caste
Writes the presidential address for the Jat-Pat-Todak Mandal conference in Lahore. The Hindu reformers cancel the event after reading the draft — it too directly challenges Hindu scripture as the root of caste. Ambedkar publishes it at his own expense, including the full correspondence with the organisers.
1942
Labour Member, Viceroy's Executive Council
Appointed as Labour Member, where he pushed for workers' rights, established the Employment Exchange system, and set the groundwork for the Employees' State Insurance Act. His economic contributions are among his least-discussed but most consequential.
1947–1951
Drafts the Indian Constitution
Appointed Law Minister and chairman of the Constitution Drafting Committee. The document he produced abolished untouchability (Article 17), guaranteed fundamental rights, established affirmative action, and enshrined equality before law — all directly targeting the caste system.
1951
Resigns from Cabinet over Hindu Code Bill
Resigned as Law Minister when Nehru's government stalled the Hindu Code Bill — which would have given Hindu women rights to divorce and inheritance. Ambedkar saw the delay as caste Hindu patriarchy overriding constitutional reform.
14 October 1956
Conversion to Buddhism — Nagpur
On Ashoka Vijaya Dashami, Ambedkar converted to Buddhism at Deekshabhoomi, Nagpur, along with approximately 600,000 followers — the largest mass religious conversion in recorded history. He chose Buddhism for its rejection of the soul (which caste-Hinduism uses to justify birth-hierarchy) and its emphasis on reason and equality.
6 December 1956
Mahaparinirvana
Ambedkar passed away in his sleep at his Delhi residence, just 47 days after his conversion. He was 65. He had been working on his final book, The Buddha and His Dhamma, which was completed and published posthumously. 6 December is observed as Mahaparinirvan Din.

The Constitution — Ambedkar's most powerful act

When India became independent, Ambedkar was appointed Law Minister and chairman of the Constitution Drafting Committee. The document he produced was designed to dismantle, from above, the caste hierarchy that had structured Indian society for millennia.

"Constitutional morality is not a natural sentiment. It has to be cultivated. We must realise that our people have yet to learn it. Democracy in India is only a top dressing on an Indian soil which is essentially undemocratic."

— B.R. Ambedkar, Constituent Assembly, 1948

Key provisions Ambedkar fought for:

Article 17 abolishes untouchability and makes its practice a punishable offence. This was the first time a nation constitutionally abolished a social practice targeting a specific community.

Articles 15 and 16 prohibit discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth, and provide for affirmative action in public employment.

Article 21 — the right to life — has been interpreted by the Supreme Court to include the right to live with dignity, a provision Ambedkar intended as a bulwark against caste-based dehumanisation.

Ambedkar resigned as Law Minister in 1951 when Nehru's government stalled the Hindu Code Bill — which would have given Hindu women rights to divorce and property inheritance. He saw the delay as a failure of constitutional morality, surrendering to caste-Hindu conservative pressure.

The conversion — 14 October 1956

After 21 years of comparative religious study, Ambedkar chose Buddhism. He spent those years studying Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, and Buddhism before concluding that Buddhism was the only major religion whose foundational philosophy was incompatible with caste hierarchy.

"I was born a Hindu. I had no choice. But I will not die a Hindu."

— B.R. Ambedkar, Yeola Conference, 1935

He chose Buddhism specifically because it rejected the concept of the atman (soul) — which caste-Hinduism uses to justify birth-based hierarchy (the soul is "born into" a varna). Without the soul, there is no justification for caste as a spiritual category.

On 14 October 1956, at Deekshabhoomi in Nagpur, he took the Three Refuges and the Five Precepts from a Sri Lankan Buddhist monk, U Chandramani. He then administered the same oath to approximately 600,000 followers — the largest mass conversion in history. He died 47 days later.

Legacy

Ambedkar's legacy operates on multiple levels simultaneously — as a constitutional architect, as an economist who shaped the Reserve Bank of India, as a social reformer who gave Dalits a political identity, and as a philosopher whose writings continue to define the terms of the debate on caste.

Bharat Ratna — India's highest civilian award — was conferred posthumously in 1990, 34 years after his death. The delay itself is a statement about how dominant-caste India viewed his legacy.

Jai Bhim — the salutation used by Ambedkarites — is now one of the most recognised political greetings in India, used by Dalits, anti-caste activists, and progressives globally.

Deekshabhoomi, Nagpur, where he converted, is now one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in India, drawing millions of Dalits each year on 14 October.

His writings continue to be suppressed, misrepresented, and removed from curricula — most notably Riddles in Hinduism, which has been banned in various states. The suppression of Ambedkar's ideas is itself evidence of their continuing relevance.

Common questions

Ambedkar was not anti-religion — he converted to Buddhism and wrote extensively on religious philosophy. He was specifically critical of caste-Hinduism's use of scripture (particularly Manusmriti and the Varna system) to justify social hierarchy. He distinguished between personal faith and institutionalised discrimination. His criticism was structural, not spiritual.
The central conflict was over the Poona Pact (1932). Ambedkar had won separate electorates for Untouchables in the Communal Award — meaning Dalits could elect their own representatives. Gandhi fasted unto death to oppose this, calling separate electorates a division of Hindus. Ambedkar was forced to sign the Poona Pact (giving up separate electorates) to save Gandhi's life — a decision he called one of the greatest betrayals of Dalit political rights. He wrote a detailed critique of Gandhi's approach in "What Congress and Gandhi Have Done to the Untouchables."
Ambedkar wrote a rigorous scholarly analysis of the partition question — "Pakistan or the Partition of India" (1940). He was not an advocate of partition in the political sense, but he analysed it as an intellectual problem with a historian's detachment. He warned about the implications for minorities on both sides of any partition. His views were complex and have been selectively quoted by both sides of the debate.
The educational curriculum in India is substantially controlled by dominant-caste interests. Ambedkar's writings — particularly Annihilation of Caste and Riddles in Hinduism — directly challenge the religious and cultural foundations that upper-caste communities use to justify their social position. The NCERT curriculum includes Ambedkar but in a sanitised, depoliticised form — focusing on his constitutional role and erasing his radical social critique. His portrait hangs in Parliament; his most important ideas are kept out of classrooms.
Navayana (literally "new vehicle") is the form of Buddhism that Ambedkar founded. It differs from Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana Buddhism in that it places social liberation and the abolition of caste at its centre. Ambedkar rejected karma as a justification for social inequality — he reinterpreted the Buddha's teachings through the lens of social justice. The Buddha and His Dhamma is the scripture of Navayana Buddhism. Today, the majority of Indian Buddhists are Ambedkarite Dalits.
Yes. Ambedkar's doctoral thesis at LSE, "The Problem of the Rupee" (1923), provided the intellectual foundation for India's central banking framework. The Hilton Young Commission, which recommended the establishment of the Reserve Bank of India in 1934, drew directly on his work. This is one of Ambedkar's contributions that is least known — that the architect of India's social justice framework also shaped its monetary architecture.
