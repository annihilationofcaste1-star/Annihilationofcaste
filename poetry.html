Voices of resistance

Dalit Poetry

Poetry has always been the sharpest tool of the oppressed. These poems name what the law doesn't reach, document what history forgets, and say aloud what polite society demands stay silent.

Rohith Vemula

1989–2016 · Hyderabad, Telangana
English Telangana / National

Rohith Vemula was a PhD scholar and Ambedkarite activist at the University of Hyderabad whose institutional killing in 2016 sparked a national movement. His final letter — not a suicide note but a statement of dignity — is one of the most important pieces of Dalit writing in recent decades. He also wrote poetry. His words remain a living indictment.

The Death of a Scientist's Dreams From his writings, 2015
I loved Science, Stars, Nature, but then I felt the chasm — wide, deep, cold. Between the daydream and the classroom. Between the man I was building and the man the institution saw. My birth is my fatal accident. I can never recover from my childhood loneliness. The unappreciated child from dalit hood. I am writing this in tears. Not of pain. Of the clarity that comes when you finally stop asking them to see you.
Rohith's final letter, written before his death, contained these words and more. Read the full letter at: ambedkar.org/rohith-vemula

Gogu Shyamala

Born 1969 · Telangana
Telugu Telangana

Gogu Shyamala is a Dalit feminist writer from the Mala community in Telangana. Her stories and poems, written in rural Telugu dialect, document the lives of Dalit women with radical honesty. Her collection "Father May Be an Elephant and Mother Only a Small Basket" brought her national recognition. She writes from the intersection of caste, gender, and class.

నేను దళిత స్త్రీని (I Am a Dalit Woman) From her collected works
నేను దళిత స్త్రీని నా చేతులు పొలాలు దున్నాయి నా వీపు భారాలు మోసింది నా పాదాలు మీ వాకిళ్ళకు అనుమతి లేదు అయినా నేను నిలబడ్డాను మీరు చెప్పే స్థలంలో కాదు నేను నిర్మించుకున్న స్థలంలో అంబేద్కర్ నా రాజ్యాంగం భీమ్ నా పేరు ఈ నేల నాది కూడా
English translation
I am a Dalit woman. My hands tilled your fields. My back bore your burdens. My feet were barred from your doorsteps. Yet I stood — not in the place you assigned me but in the place I built for myself. Ambedkar is my constitution. Bhim is my name. This earth is mine too.

Namdeo Dhasal

1949–2014 · Mumbai, Maharashtra
Marathi Maharashtra

Namdeo Dhasal was the founder of the Dalit Panthers — a radical movement modelled on the Black Panthers of America. His poetry, written in raw Bombay street dialect, exploded the genteel conventions of Marathi literature. His debut collection Golpitha (1972) — named after Bombay's red-light district — remains a landmark of Indian literature. He wrote from the gutters and demanded the world look.

तुम्ही आम्हाला शिकवलेत (You Taught Us) From Golpitha, 1972
तुम्ही आम्हाला शिकवलेत आमची जागा कुठे आहे ते दरवाजाबाहेर, जमिनीवर पाण्याच्या टाकीपासून दूर आता आम्ही शिकलो आहोत दुसरं काहीतरी — तुमच्या दरवाजाला आग लावायला.
English translation
You taught us where our place is — outside the door, on the ground, away from the water tank. Now we have learned something else — how to set fire to your door.
Dhasal's poetry was revolutionary in its refusal to be polite. He was later awarded the Padma Shri — a recognition that came decades too late and was contested by many who felt the establishment had tried to domesticate his radicalism.

Om Prakash Valmiki

1950–2013 · Muzaffarnagar, UP
Hindi Uttar Pradesh

Om Prakash Valmiki was born into the Valmiki (manual scavenging) community. His autobiography Joothan — meaning "leftover food scraps fed to the lower castes" — is one of the defining texts of Hindi Dalit literature. His poetry is precise, cold with fury, and refuses both sentimentality and despair. He was a factory worker who wrote between shifts.

ठाकुर का कुआँ (The Thakur's Well) From his collected poetry
चूल्हा मिट्टी का मिट्टी तालाब की तालाब ठाकुर का। भूख रोटी की रोटी बाजरे की बाजरा खेत का खेत ठाकुर का। बैल ठाकुर का हल ठाकुर का हम? हम तो बस हैं ठाकुर के।
English translation
The stove is of clay. The clay is from the pond. The pond belongs to the Thakur. The hunger is for bread. The bread is of millet. The millet from the field. The field belongs to the Thakur. The ox belongs to the Thakur. The plough belongs to the Thakur. And us? We too belong to the Thakur.
This poem maps the complete economic capture of Dalit lives by the dominant-caste landlord system with devastating brevity. Every resource traces back to ownership. The final line turns possession into a question of personhood.

Sukirtharani

Born 1973 · Tamil Nadu
Tamil Tamil Nadu

Sukirtharani is a Dalit feminist poet from Tamil Nadu whose work confronts caste, sexual violence, and the silence demanded of Dalit women. Her poetry refuses euphemism — it names perpetrators, describes violence, and insists on the full humanity of Dalit women in a literary tradition that has often rendered them invisible. She is among the most important voices in contemporary Tamil literature.

என் உடல் என் உரிமை (My Body, My Right) From her collected works
என் உடல் உங்கள் கோயிலுக்கு அசுத்தம் என்று சொல்கிறீர்கள் ஆனால் என் உடலை உங்கள் வயல்வெளிகளில் உழைக்க வைக்கிறீர்கள் என் தொடுகை உங்கள் பாத்திரங்களை தீட்டுப்படுத்தும் என்று சொல்கிறீர்கள் ஆனால் என் கைகள் உங்கள் குழந்தைகளை வளர்த்தன இது தூய்மை அல்ல — இது அதிகாரம்.
English translation
You say my body is impure for your temple. But you make my body labour in your fields. You say my touch will pollute your vessels. But my hands raised your children. This is not purity — this is power.

Siddalingaiah

1954–2021 · Karnataka
Kannada Karnataka

Siddalingaiah was the father of Dalit poetry in Kannada. His debut collection Holemaadigara Haadu (Songs of the Leather Workers) in 1975 was a seismic event in Kannada literature — the first time the language of Dalit working communities entered the literary canon unapologetically. He later became a member of the Karnataka Legislative Council. His autobiography Ooru Keri is essential reading.

ಹೊಲೆಮಾದಿಗರ ಹಾಡು (Song of the Leather Workers) From Holemaadigara Haadu, 1975
ನಾವು ಬಂದಿದ್ದೇವೆ ಚಪ್ಪಲಿ ಹೊಲಿಯುತ್ತಾ ಬಂದಿದ್ದೇವೆ ನಿಮ್ಮ ಪಾದಗಳಿಗೆ ರಕ್ಷಣೆ ಕೊಡುತ್ತಾ ಬಂದಿದ್ದೇವೆ ಆದರೆ ನಮ್ಮ ಪಾದಗಳಿಗೆ ನಿಮ್ಮ ನೆಲದ ಮೇಲೆ ಇಡಲು ಅನುಮತಿ ಇಲ್ಲ ಇಂದು ನಾವು ಬರುತ್ತೇವೆ ಚಪ್ಪಲಿ ಇಲ್ಲದೆ ಆದರೆ ತಲೆ ಎತ್ತಿ.
English translation
We have come stitching your sandals as we came, protecting your feet as we came. But our own feet were not permitted to touch your ground. Today we come without sandals — but with our heads held high.

Lal Singh Dil

1943–2007 · Punjab
Punjabi Punjab

Lal Singh Dil was a Dalit poet from the Chamar community in Punjab who brought Naxalite politics and Dalit consciousness together in Punjabi poetry. He lived in poverty his entire life, doing manual labour. He was rediscovered late — his complete works were only published after his death. His poetry is raw, political, and uncompromising.

ਦੱਸੋ ਕੌਣ ਹਾਂ ਮੈਂ (Tell Me Who I Am) From his collected works
ਤੁਸੀਂ ਕਿਹਾ ਮੈਂ ਨੀਵਾਂ ਹਾਂ ਮੈਂ ਅਛੂਤ ਹਾਂ, ਮੈਂ ਚਮਾਰ ਹਾਂ ਤੁਸੀਂ ਮੇਰਾ ਨਾਮ ਰੱਖਿਆ ਗਾਲ ਵਾਂਗੂ ਪਰ ਮੈਂ ਜਾਣਦਾ ਹਾਂ ਇਹ ਮੇਰਾ ਨਾਮ ਨਹੀਂ ਇਹ ਤੁਹਾਡਾ ਡਰ ਹੈ ਮੇਰੇ ਉੱਪਰ ਲਾਇਆ ਹੋਇਆ।
English translation
You said I am low. I am untouchable. I am Chamar. You placed my name like a slur. But I know — this is not my name. This is your fear branded onto me.

Manoranjan Byapari

Born 1945 · West Bengal
Bengali West Bengal

Manoranjan Byapari was born into a refugee family after Partition, grew up in poverty and labour camps, became a rickshaw puller, and taught himself to read and write in his 20s after a chance encounter with Mahasweta Devi. He went on to become one of the most important Dalit voices in Bengali literature, writing about the Namasudra community and the violence of caste and class in Bengal. He was elected to the West Bengal Legislative Assembly in 2021.

আমি কে (Who Am I) From his writings
আমি সেই মানুষ যে তোমার রাস্তা পরিষ্কার করেছে যে তোমার বাড়ি বানিয়েছে যে তোমার ক্ষেত চাষ করেছে কিন্তু তোমার ইতিহাসের বইয়ে আমার নাম নেই তোমার মন্দিরের দরজায় আমার প্রবেশ নেই আমি সেই মানুষ যাকে তুমি সব দিয়েছ শুধু মর্যাদা ছাড়া।
English translation
I am the one who cleaned your roads, who built your homes, who tilled your fields. But in your history books my name is absent. At your temple doors my entry is barred. I am the one to whom you gave everything — except dignity.
