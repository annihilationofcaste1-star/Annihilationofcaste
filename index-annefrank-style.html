The speech they would not let him deliver.

In 1936, a Hindu reform society invited Ambedkar to be the president of their annual conference in Lahore.

He prepared a speech. When the organisers read the draft, they were disturbed. Ambedkar was not merely asking Hindus to reform their behaviour — he was telling them their scriptures themselves were the source of caste. They asked him to soften the text.

He refused. The conference was cancelled. He published the speech himself at his own expense — including the full correspondence with the organisers — so the world would know not only what he had written, but why it was suppressed.

Nearly ninety years later, Annihilation of Caste remains one of the most important political texts ever written in India. Its argument — that caste cannot be reformed, only destroyed — continues to provoke and to clarify.

His Life

A life in six moments.

Sixty-five years of struggle, scholarship, and refusal. A full biography awaits in the Wiki — these are the turning points.

1891
Born in Mhow
Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar is born on 14 April in the Central Provinces, the fourteenth child of a Mahar family. His early schooling is marked by the daily humiliations of untouchability — made to sit separately, denied water, denied dignity.
1915 – 1923
Columbia and LSE
Earns PhD from Columbia University (1917), MSc and DSc from the London School of Economics, and is called to the Bar at Gray's Inn — becoming one of the most educated Indians of his generation, and the first Dalit to achieve this level of international scholarship.
1927
Mahad Satyagraha
Leads thousands of Dalits in the Mahad Satyagraha, asserting their right to drink water from a public tank. Burns the Manusmriti — the Hindu text codifying caste hierarchy — in an act that becomes a symbol of the Dalit movement.
1936
Annihilation of Caste
Writes the speech that Hindu reformers will cancel before he can deliver it. Publishes it himself — along with the full suppression correspondence. The text becomes one of the most influential political works in South Asian history.
1947 – 1951
Architect of the Constitution
As Law Minister and chairman of the Drafting Committee, authors the Indian Constitution — enshrining Article 17's abolition of untouchability, fundamental rights, and affirmative action. Resigns in 1951 when the government stalls the Hindu Code Bill on women's rights.
1956
The Great Conversion
On 14 October, at Deekshabhoomi in Nagpur, embraces Buddhism along with 600,000 followers — the largest mass religious conversion in history. Dies 47 days later, on 6 December, while completing his final work, The Buddha and His Dhamma.
