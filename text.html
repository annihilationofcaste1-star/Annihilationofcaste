B.R. Ambedkar · 1936

Annihilation
of Caste

The speech that was never delivered.

Why this text exists

In 1936, the Jat-Pat-Todak Mandal — a Hindu reformist organisation in Lahore — invited Ambedkar to be the president of their annual conference. He prepared a speech. When the organisers read it in advance, they were disturbed by its content and demanded he revise it. Ambedkar refused.

The conference was cancelled. Ambedkar published the speech himself at his own expense — including the full correspondence with the organisers. It became one of the most widely read political texts in South Asian history. The suppression became part of the text's power.

A timeline

1930
Ambedkar leads the first Dalit rights conference at Nagpur, demanding equal access to public water sources.
1935
Ambedkar publicly declares he will not die a Hindu — beginning his 21-year study of world religions.
1936
Writes Annihilation of Caste as a presidential address for the Jat-Pat-Todak Mandal conference in Lahore. The conference is cancelled. He publishes it himself.
1936
Gandhi responds in Harijan magazine, disagreeing with Ambedkar's critique of the Hindu scriptures. Ambedkar publishes a sharp, detailed rejoinder.
1947
Ambedkar chairs the drafting committee of the Indian Constitution — encoding fundamental rights that directly challenged caste hierarchy.
1956
14 October, Nagpur. Ambedkar converts to Buddhism along with 600,000 followers — the largest mass religious conversion in history.
2014
Verso Books publishes a new annotated edition with an introduction by Arundhati Roy, bringing Annihilation of Caste to a global English-language audience.

"You cannot build anything on the foundation of caste. You cannot build up a nation, you cannot build up a morality. Anything you will build on the foundation of caste will crack and will never be a whole."

— Annihilation of Caste, Part III

"Turn in any direction you like, caste is the monster that crosses your path. You cannot have political reform, you cannot have economic reform, unless you kill this monster."

— Annihilation of Caste, Part I

"The Hindus claim to be a very tolerant people. In my opinion this is a mistake. On many occasions they can be intolerant and if on some occasions they are tolerant, that is because they are indifferent."

— Annihilation of Caste, Part II

Read the full text

The complete text of Annihilation of Caste is freely available from multiple public sources. We recommend the annotated Verso edition (2014) which includes Ambedkar's correspondence with the Jat-Pat-Todak Mandal and a comprehensive introduction.
